Finland

Introduction

Finland began as a province, and then grand duchy, under Sweden from 12th to 19th centuries, and an autonomous grand duchy under Russia after 1809. It gained complete independece in 1917, and successfully defended its independence during World War II with Germany.

In the past 50 years, Finland has transformed from an agricultural economy to a more diverse industrial economy, lending to its per capita income becoming one of the highest in Western Europe.

In the 21st century, the key features of Finland's modern welfare state are high quality education, promotion of equality, and a national social welfare system. Its current challenges are an aging, older population and the fluctuations of an export-driven economy.

Geography

Location

Finland lies within Northern Europe, bordered by the Baltic Sea, Gulf of Bothnia and Gulf of Finland. It borders Norway (709 km), Sweden (545 km) and Russia (1309 km).

Climate and Terrain

Currently, Finland is the 66th largest country in the world. Its area is approximately the size of Montana, though slightly smaller.

The terrain is relatively flat, with rolling plains and low hills littered with more than 60,000 freshwater lakes. Its major lakes, in order of size are:

  1. Lake Saimaa (1,760 sq km)
  2. Lake Paijanne (1,090 sq km)
  3. Lake Inarijarvi (1,000 sq km)
  4. Lake Oulujarvi (900 sq km)
  5. Lake Pielinen (850 sq km)

Finland has a primarily cold-temperate climate, and at times, approaching potential subarctic levels; much of Finland's northern regions are within the Arctic Circle. Otherwise, the country is considered fairly mild with the temperature moderation from the Baltic Sea, the North Atlantic Current, and tens of thousands of inner lakes. Natural hazards are severe winter storms in the north.

Population Distribution

Finland's total population is 5,587,442 (July 2021 est).

The vast majority of the population is settled in southern Finland, concentrated on a small southwestern coastal plain. The northern interior areas remain sparsely populated.

People and Society

Nationality

People from Finland are referred to as 'Finns' (noun). When describing something of Finland's origin, it's 'Finnish' (adj).

Finland's Independence Day is December 6th. Its national symbol is the lion, and its national colors are blue and white.

Ethnic Groups and Languages

92% of the total population has a Finnish background. Other ethnicities include Swedish, Russian, Estonian, Romani, and Sami.

Demographic

About 85.7% of the total population lives in urban areas, and more than 1.3 million citizens live in the capital of Helsinki alone.

The Finnish population trends older, with 35.28% of the population being older than 55 years old, and only 27.36% younger than 25 years old.

Military and Security

Military and Security Forces

The Finnish Defense Forces (FDF) is comprised of three branches and one conditional branch:

  • Army (Maavoimat)
  • Navy (Merivoimat)
  • Air Force (Ilmavoimat)

The Ministry of the Interior Border Guard (Rajavartiolaitos) becomes part of the FDF during wartime.

Service Age and Obligation

Finland trains approximately 21,000 conscripts each year. All Finnish men are required to serve within a branch of the military or the Border Guard (depending on the type of duty) at 18 years old. The table below details the military requirements and options for Finnish citizens.

Service Age and Requirements by Sex
Sex Age Required? Length of Service
Male Upon turning 18+ Yes 5.5-12 months
Female 18 - 29 No Optional

Note: There is also an option to perform non-military service for 8.5 or 11.5 months.

As of 2019, women made up about 4% of the military's full-time personnel.

After completing their initial conscript obligation, individuals enter the reserves and stay eligible for mobilization until the age of 50 for rank-and-file and 60 for non-commissioned and commissioned officers.

Active Support Operations

The Finnish Armed Forces closely cooperate with the militaries of other Nordic countries through the Nordic Defense Cooperation (NORDEFCO), which consists of Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, and Sweden; these countries work within a voluntary participation model to cooperate in areas through armaments, education, human resources, training and exercises, and operations. This is not a military alliance.

Finland is not a member of NATO, but the two actively cooperate in peace-support operations, exercise together, and exchange analysis and information; Finland joined NATO’s Partnership for Peace program in 1994; Finnish Armed Forces participated in NATO-led military operations and missions in the Balkans, Afghanistan, and Iraq

Finland is a signatory of the EU’s Common Security and Defense Policy (CSDP) and actively participates in CSDP crisis management missions and operations
