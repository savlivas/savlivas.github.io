My 2022 Resolution - Complete Pokemon Card Organization (Sav Olivas)

Given the recent boom in the Pokemon card economy, I figured I'd leaf through ten years' worth of my collection this year to complete a comprehensive catalogue of the ones I own. Ranging from 2002 (so far) to 2021, I approximate I have over 1,000 cards!

Current Work

As of February 1st, I have catalogued 116 Pokemon, and have finished the Kanto region of my collection (116/151 Kanto Pokemon). In total, I have sorted 280 individual cards.

My rarest find is a Machop from the 2002 Legendary Collection. This card is a Common (Reverse Holographic), and averages a $40 selling price on the online market.

General Process

I've made two separate databases in Microsoft Excel. My primary catalogue consists of the cards sorted as they are in my binder-- Pokedex number. My secondary catalogue list focuses on each of the different collections that were released over the years; after categorizing each region in the primary log, I record the cards I have in each set in the secondary lists. This might seen confusing at first (especially without a visual guide), but I'll detail more below, and hopefully it'll make more sense.

The Primary Database

Using sites like PokemonTCG and cardmavin, I've sorted each individual card by its collection set number, year, artist and rarity. This is to take having multiple different cards for the same Pokemon into account.

For example, in one row of a page in my binder, I have the following cards:

  1. #004 Charmander (1 card)
  2. #005 Charmeleon (2 cards)
  3. #006 Charizard (2 cards)

Though it may look like an easy catalogue to make, there's more that goes into catagorizing different Pokemon cards. Here's an example of what these cards would look like in my primary database:

Although I have two Charmeleon cards and two Charizard cards, they have different illustrations, rarities, and come from separate set releases. My two Charizards have to be treated as two separate cards, despite the fact they depict the same Pokemon.

For some Pokemon, I might have more than 10 different cards, so I methodically log each one under these classifications to best display the full variety I own. After all, a collector of a certain set might just need a single card from my dozens of variants, and I should know whether I have that specific Pokemon from that specific set before cutting a deal.

The Secondary Database

This leads into my secondary database; a complete inventory of the cards I have for every released set of Pokemon cards. This is only an easy feat after the initial primary categorization -- 25 years of card releases is best tackled piece by piece. For this set, I only begin marking down Pokemon cards after I've finished one Pokemon region in the primary set; the Pokedex numbers serve as that designation.

If you know anything about Pokemon, some of this might not seem right. None of these correspond to the individual Pokemon's official Pokedex number (Charmeleon is #005 in the Pokedex). Some evolutions are separated (i.e. Gastly and Haunter). This is because the (X/99) numbers correspond to the rarity of the cards, listed in alphabetical order. These cards are all in the 'Uncommon' rarity section.

Though it might not look like I have many in this collection, it's likely I have cards from later generations to fill in those gaps! Time and effort will tell!
